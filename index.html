Curator's note — what you will encounter inside

These sites are deliberately left as they were built, imperfections included. Restoring them any further would mean destroying what they are: honest documents of how the web looked and worked twenty-some years ago. So, before you go in:

  • Nothing is responsive. These pages were laid out in fixed-width tables for 800×600 CRT monitors, a decade before smartphones. On a phone they render zoomed-out and small — pinch and zoom to read. That is not a bug; that is 2002.
  • Some images are broken forever. A photo on the me & javier page was never captured by the Wayback Machine and no other copy exists. One page of the DTM site (euro1.htm) is lost the same way.
  • Some things were broken on day one. The mummy & daddy photo has pointed at C:\My Documents\ since 2002 — FrontPage kept my local disk path when publishing. A page on the DTM site does the same with drive H:. Preserved with affection.
  • Most outbound links are dead. The guestbook service, the counters, the partner sites of 2001–2006 — nearly all gone. The links stay, pointing at ghosts; paste them into web.archive.org if you're curious.
  • Internet Explorer assumptions. One site politely informs you it is “best viewed in Microsoft Internet Explorer,” and FrontPage hover animations silently do nothing in your modern browser.

What was repaired, sparingly: the free hosts' injected ad scripts were removed, the text was transcoded to UTF-8 so Turkish renders on modern hosting, and the dead hit counter and news-headline widget were rebuilt to work again. Every intervention is documented, commit by commit, in the repository — the untouched originals live in its history.

Every page here is served exactly as it was written, give or take the ad scripts its free hosts injected. The recovery process — crawling the Wayback Machine, stripping dead third-party code, reviving the hit counter and news widget — is documented in the repository.
